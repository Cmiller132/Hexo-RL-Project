## Pair-native search

Treating the post-opening move as an unordered two-cell action makes sense for Hexo at the semantic level. The game guide says that after the opening, each turn consists of two placements, that 4-windows and 5-windows are both immediate one-turn threats, and that unblockability is fundamentally about whether *any pair of defensive placements* can cover the opponentŌĆÖs hot windows. In other words, the core tactical object in Hexo is already pair-shaped. A sequential tree can represent that, but it represents it through an artificial ordered decomposition whose statistics are attached to half-moves rather than to the real turn. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file1ŅłéL17-L28Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file1ŅłéL46-L95Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL202-L230Ņłü

So yes: **pair-native MCTS is likely better than sequential single-placement MCTS once the proposal system is mature**. The reason is not elegance. The reason is that HexoŌĆÖs high-value actions often come from pair interaction: double blocks, paired extensions, cover-sharing, overload creation, and multi-window pressure. Sampled-action planning methods are explicitly motivated by settings where the true action space is too large to enumerate but the algorithm should still reason over sampled action subsets in a principled way, and the Gumbel paper is directly about improving root policy when not all actions are visited. That is exactly the shape of this problem. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL215-L230Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn0search1Ņłéturn0search5Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search6Ņłéturn0search0Ņłü

But I would not oversell it. Pair-native search is *not* automatically better than sequential DAG search. It is better only if the selector almost always admits the tactically relevant or strategically dominant pair, and if the runtime does not lose too much wall-clock on candidate generation, deduplication, reranking, and metadata bookkeeping. The document is right to insist that the real acceptance test is equal wall-clock against a fair sequential DAG baseline, not equal simulations. That is the correct standard. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL113-L115Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1572-L1574Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1580-L1608Ņłü

There is, however, one rule-level issue that the plan must resolve before I would sign off on the move contract. The game guide says a stone may be placed on an empty hex within distance 8 of **any existing stone**. On the natural reading of that rule, the first stone of the turn becomes an existing stone before the second placement, which means the second stone may be legal even if it was *not* legal at the start of the turn. The implementation plan, by contrast, repeatedly speaks as if both cells in a `PairAction` are legal in the current full-turn state and as if pair rows simply reference current `LEGAL` rows. If the first stone can expand the legal frontier, then ŌĆ£unordered pair of two start-of-turn legal cellsŌĆØ is not the full move space, and the runtime contract is malformed. The document hints at ŌĆ£known-first second-placement rows,ŌĆØ which suggests the authors know this issue exists, but it never resolves it. This is not bookkeeping. It changes the action space. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file1ŅłéL23-L25Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL360-L385Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL841-L842Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL954-L955Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1234-L1245Ņłü

That means the correct verdict on pair-native search is: **yes, pair-native is the right semantic target, but the plan must first settle whether pair legality is frozen at turn start or requires an execution-order witness for the second stone**. If the second stone can be unlocked by the first, the right abstraction may be ŌĆ£unordered final pair plus one legal realization order,ŌĆØ not a plain unordered pair over start-of-turn legal rows. Until that is written down and tested in Rust fixtures, the central action contract is incomplete. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL381-L385Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1234-L1254Ņłü

## Model-head review

The head set is mostly sensible, but it is too large for a V1 that is already changing action semantics, search, replay, data contracts, and tactical labeling at the same time. My high-level judgment is: keep the dense auxiliary heads that directly support candidate recall, keep the value head, keep the exact tactical head, keep the final bounded-pool pair scorer, and demote the search-distillation and uncertainty heads out of the required V1 contract. **`cell_marginal_logits`** are useful and should stay. The right target is the marginal induced by the pair posterior, not a regression back to ŌĆ£single-placement policy.ŌĆØ ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL184-L185Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL893-L917Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL993-L999Ņłü

**`pair_completion_logits`** are useful but risky. Conceptually they are sound: anchor-conditioned completion is a natural way to cover large variable action sets, and pointer-style conditional selection is exactly the kind of thing Pointer Networks were designed for. The plan explicitly says to train both unordered conditional views, which is correct. I would keep this head, but make it minimal and pragmatic in V1. Do not over-engineer it. ## Candidate generation and search runtime

The source-based candidate selector is conceptually excellent. In large action spaces, ŌĆ£just sample from the policyŌĆØ is a bootstrap trap and ŌĆ£just score everythingŌĆØ is a runtime trap. A source-tagged mixture is the right compromise because it gives you diversity, explicit tactical protection, auditability, and a way to detect support collapse. The document is strongest where it insists that candidate generation should be recall-oriented before it is precision-oriented. That is the right mindset. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL621-L691Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL693-L759Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn0search1Ņłéturn0search5Ņłü

`direct_pair_retrieval` is, in my view, the single most important technical addition beyond ŌĆ£make pair the semantic action.ŌĆØ It addresses the pair-native failure mode directly: some strong pairs are good *because of the relation*, not because either endpoint is individually salient. The plan describes exactly that motivation, and it is correct. A cheap symmetric low-rank proposal score over legal-cell embeddings is the right sort of retrieval mechanism. If this head works, pair-native search becomes viable. If it does not, the whole design becomes much more dependent on brittle cross-products and completions. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL761-L790Ņłü

The choice to require **blockwise-exact retrieval before ANN** is also right. In V1, reproducibility, D6 stability, diagonal masking, order invariance, and retrieval debugging matter more than shaving the last constant factor. Approximate retrieval is a performance optimization; exact retrieval is a correctness and observability tool. The planŌĆÖs staged position here is very good. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL792-L814Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1278-L1335Ņłü

The recall metrics and source ablations are substantial, but they are not yet sufficient. The plan says to measure recall against exhaustive tiny-state search and large-budget audit search, and that is necessary. What is still missing is a cleaner *definition* of recall targets. You need at least four distinct metrics: recall of exact tactical winners/blocks; recall of the final deep-search best pair; recall of an equivalence class when multiple terminal pairs are strategically identical; and recall of cumulative deep-search probability mass within the admitted pool, not just top-1 containment. You also need to report those numbers separately for the pre-candidate pool and the final admitted set, and separately at root and interior nodes. Otherwise the selector, reranker, and widening logic can each hide the othersŌĆÖ failures. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1311-L1321Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1525-L1545Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1584-L1593Ņłü

Gumbel sequential halving at the root makes sense. This is one place where the external literature maps directly onto the design. Danihelka et al. argue that AlphaZero-style training can fail to improve the policy if the root search does not visit all actions, and they replace heuristic root handling with a proper policy-improvement operator that works especially well with few simulations. That is highly relevant to a sampled-pair root where candidate sets may still be larger than the simulation budget. The plan is right to want a real improved root posterior rather than raw visit counts over a sampled set. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL569-L594Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search6Ņłéturn0search0Ņłü

Proposal-aware PUCT below the root also makes sense in principle, but this is where the plan becomes mathematically weak. The document requires a combined `beta` or inclusion estimate per deduplicated candidate and then uses that estimate to correct priors. That is fine if the selector is a well-defined stochastic sampler. But many of the proposed sources are deterministic top-k mechanisms, quota fillers, or unions of both. Once you mix deterministic retrieval, quota-based diversity, tactical protection, and canary sampling, a single scalar `beta` stops being a clean probability and starts becoming a propensity heuristic. The plan currently treats that distinction too casually. My recommendation is to replace the one-field idea of `combined_beta` with explicit metadata: exact inclusion probability when it exists, heuristic propensity when it does not, and a correction mode flag that prevents the runtime from pretending a heuristic is an importance weight. Progressive widening is conceptually correct but not operationally specified well enough. The document gives the widening formula, which is fine, but it does not clearly define what happens when a node first expands and later widens. The right V1 answer is: **one model call when a node is first expanded; cache legal embeddings, proposal outputs, and a ranked candidate reservoir; then reveal more children from that cached reservoir as visits grow**. Re-running the network or rebuilding a large selector pool at every widening event will kill wall-clock. ## Performance and migration from the current system

The likely performance bottleneck is not just neural inference. It is the entire CPU-side and cross-language pipeline around each expanded node: legal-row construction, canonicalization, tactical labeling, pair retrieval, source union, deduplication, proposal metadata, bounded reranking, node storage, and widening. The plan understands this better than many documents do, which is good. But the scale pressure is real. Even under the conservative interpretation where the first post-opening legal set is only the 216 empty cells within radius 8 of the origin, there are already 23,220 unordered pairs. And if the rules allow the first stone to expand second-stone legality, the true turn action space is larger still. That is why exhaustive pair scoring per node is dead on arrival and why the selector architecture matters so much. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file1ŅłéL17-L25Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL211-L213Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL683-L685Ņłü

The good news is that the planŌĆÖs *shape* is right for avoiding hundreds of neural calls per MCTS leaf. It explicitly says to encode once at a full-turn node, produce legal-cell embeddings and proposal outputs, build a bounded pre-candidate pool, rerank only that pool, and then widen over admitted pair children. That is exactly what you want. If implemented faithfully, the net should be called once per newly expanded node, not once per visited child. That is plausible. The danger is not the main algorithm; the danger is accidental implementation drift where widening, rescoring, or target metadata extraction causes repeated model or selector work. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL543-L567Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL672-L685Ņłü ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn1file0ŅłéL1361-L1364Ņłü

The proposed vectorized scoring and candidate budgets are plausible *only in a disciplined regime*. A cheap blockwise pair retrieval over legal embeddings is mathematically just a sequence of block matrix products plus masking, which is tractable. A rich bounded-pool reranker over a few hundred pairs is also tractable. What is not tractable is letting interior nodes rebuild thousand-pair pools repeatedly or letting the rich scorer quietly expand toward all-pairs behavior because the candidate budgets grew under pressure. The document needs concrete starting budgets by phase, by root/interior distinction, and by legal-pair-count bucket. Without those, ŌĆ£boundedŌĆØ is aspiration, not engineering. ## Missing pieces and required changes before approval
